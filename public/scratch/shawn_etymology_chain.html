The etymology of Shawn
Seven forms across five languages — newest at top, oldest at bottom
English
Shawnalt: Shaun, Shon
Pron."SHAWN" (rhymes with dawn)
Eramid-20th c. CE
MeaningGod is gracious
RegionUS & UK
Purely phonetic respelling of Irish Seán for English readers who found the native spelling counterintuitive. Peaked in US popularity 1965–1985.
↑
Phonetic anglicization of Irish Seán, 20th c.
Irish
Seánalt: Sean (without fada)
Pron."SHAWN" (the fada lengthens the vowel)
Era~13th c. CE onward
MeaningGod is gracious
RegionIreland
Irish phonology transformed the Anglo-Norman name brought by the 1169 invasion — the initial J shifted to Sh, and the vowel lengthened. The fada (á) marks the long vowel. One of the most common male names in Ireland.
↑
Anglo-Norman settlers brought their name to Ireland (Norman invasion, 1169 CE); Irish phonology reshaped it
French
Jeanmodern standard French
Pron."ZHAHN" (soft J, nasal vowel)
Era~13th–14th c. CE
MeaningGod is gracious
RegionFrance
The contracted modern form, after the unstressed middle syllable of Jehan dropped away. The French nasal vowel (an) has no equivalent in English — the closest approximation is "ZHAHN."
↑
Middle vowel of Jehan eroded in spoken Old/Middle French, ~12th–13th c.
Old French
Jehanalt: Johan
Pron."zhuh-HAHN" (two syllables)
Era~11th–13th c. CE
MeaningGod is gracious
RegionNorthern France / Normandy
The Norman French form carried to Ireland in 1169. Direct ancestor of Irish Seán, not the later contracted "Jean." The initial J already had a soft-Zh quality in Norman dialect.
↑
Old French adapted Latin Johannes, shedding inflectional ending, ~10th–11th c.
Medieval Latin
Johannesalt: Iohannes (classical)
Pron."yo-HAH-nes" (J as English Y)
Era4th c. CE onward
MeaningGod is gracious
RegionChurch Latin / Western Europe
The standard Latin form used in Jerome's Vulgate Bible (~405 CE), from which virtually all Western European variants derive — John, Juan, Giovanni, Johann, Jan, and Jean among them.
↑
Jerome's Latin Vulgate transliterated Greek into Latin, ~4th c. CE
Koine Greek
IoannesἸωάννης
Pron."ee-oh-AH-nes"
Era~3rd c. BCE onward
MeaningGod is gracious
RegionEastern Mediterranean
Phonetic rendering of Hebrew Yohanan in the Septuagint (~3rd–2nd c. BCE) and later the New Testament. Koine was the common Greek dialect of the Hellenistic world — distinct from Classical Attic Greek.

Septuagint scribes transliterated Hebrew into Koine Greek, ~3rd–2nd c. BCE
Biblical Hebrew
Yohananיוֹחָנָן
Pron."yo-HAH-nan" (stress on 2nd syllable)
Era~6th c. BCE or earlier
MeaningGod is gracious
RegionAncient Judah / Israel
Compound of Yah (short form of YHWH) + ḥānan (to show grace or favor). Appears in the Hebrew Bible; common in Second Temple Judaism. Reads right-to-left: yod–waw–ḥet–nun–nun.

A note on sources
There is some scholarly debate as to which precise French form the Anglo-Norman invaders brought to Ireland in 1169 — and therefore which form directly preceded Irish Seán. The Online Etymology Dictionary traces the name through the Old French forms Jan, Jean, Jehan without clearly distinguishing which was current at the time of the invasion. Wikipedia's entry on Sean notes the name was adopted into Irish "most likely from Jean," yet elsewhere in the same article attributes the transmission to the Norman form Jehan or Johan. The most defensible position is that the Normans carried the two-syllable Jehan — since the contracted Jean emerged somewhat later — but the historical record is not precise enough to settle the matter definitively. The broader chain (Hebrew → Greek → Latin → French → Irish → English) is not in dispute.
Sources consulted: Online Etymology Dictionary (etymonline.com) · Wikipedia: Sean · Wikipedia: Jean
